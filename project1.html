Unequal Streets

Mapping Crime and Public Safety Disparities
Across Washington, D.C.'s Wards in 2025

Project Overview

Washington, D.C. is a city of sharp contrasts — some wards are wealthy and well-resourced while others have faced decades of disinvestment. This project uses the DC Metropolitan Police Department's 2025 Crime Incidents dataset to explore how crime type and frequency differ across the eight wards and what that reveals about inequality in public safety.

  • Course: MATH 014 – Introduction to Data Science, Howard University
  • Semester: Spring 2026
  • Dataset: DC Open Data — Crime Incidents in 2025 (24,000+ records)
  • Tools: Python, pandas, NumPy, Matplotlib, Seaborn, Plotly

Research Questions

  1. Which wards experience the highest overall crime rates in 2025?
  2. How does the distribution of offense types vary across wards?
  3. Are violent crimes concentrated in specific wards?
  4. Does the time of day interact with ward-level crime patterns?
  5. What is the relationship between offense method (gun, knife, other) and ward?

Key Findings

Wards 7 and 8 bear a disproportionate burden of violent crime. Although Wards 2 and 5 have the highest raw crime counts, Ward 2's numbers are almost entirely driven by theft in commercial and tourist areas. Wards 7 and 8 — located east of the Anacostia River — have the highest share of violent crime and the most gun-related incidents, reflecting decades of systemic disinvestment in these communities.

Fewer than 5% of crimes are homicides or sex abuse, but they are not random. Homicide is almost exclusively a Ward 7 and 8 phenomenon. Gun violence geographically clusters in the eastern part of the city, with very few incidents in the northwest — a pattern that closely mirrors DC's socioeconomic divide.

Crime is seasonal. Incidents rise in spring, peak in summer, and drop in colder months — a pattern consistent with what researchers see in cities across the country.

Theft dominates citywide. Theft/other and theft from auto account for nearly 70% of all incidents across every ward. This means most of what inflates DC's overall crime numbers is property crime, not violence.

Visualizations

The project produced 8 visualizations including:

  • Bar chart of overall offense type distribution across DC
  • Ward-by-ward total crime comparison
  • Violent vs. property crime breakdown by ward
  • Heatmap of offense type frequency across all eight wards
  • Crime volume by time of day (shift) and ward
  • Interactive stacked bar chart of offense types by ward
  • Interactive geographic map of gun-related crime locations
  • Violent crime as a percentage of total crime by ward

Limitations & Future Directions

No population data was available, so per-capita crime rates could not be calculated — a key limitation when comparing wards of different sizes. The dataset also only captures reported crimes, and reporting rates may vary by ward. Future work could merge this data with census demographics to enable proper per-capita comparisons, or use a multi-year dataset to examine whether these disparities are growing or shrinking over time.

Data Source
