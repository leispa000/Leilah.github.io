What Makes a Song Go Viral?
Analyzing Spotify Audio Features & Popularity

Project Overview

Spotify serves over 600 million users worldwide, and its recommendation engine shapes what billions of people listen to every day. But what actually makes a song popular? This project uses Spotify's own machine-learning-derived audio metrics — danceability, energy, valence, tempo, and more — to explore what separates a hit from the noise across 114,000 tracks and 114 genres.

  • Course: MATH 014 – Introduction to Data Science Honors, Howard University
  • Team: AI Innovators | Spring 2026
  • Dataset: Spotify Tracks Dataset — 114,000 tracks, 114 genres
  • Tools: Python, pandas, NumPy
  • Source: Kaggle — Spotify Tracks Dataset

Central Question

What audio features most strongly predict a song's popularity on Spotify?

Key Findings

Popularity is rare. The average track scores only 33 out of 100 on Spotify's popularity scale, and fewer than 5% of songs ever break a score of 70. Truly viral hits are the extreme exception, not the rule.

Danceability and energy help, but don't explain everything. Many highly danceable tracks still have low popularity scores, suggesting that genre, artist reputation, and recency matter just as much as audio features. A high "grooviness score" — a custom metric combining danceability and energy — identifies banger tracks, but popularity requires more than just being upbeat.

The dataset skews major and fast. About 64% of tracks are in a major key, and the average tempo is ~122 BPM — consistent with the dominance of pop and commercial music. G and C are the most common keys, aligning with music theory knowledge about beginner-friendly and widely used keys.

Only 8.5% of tracks are explicit. The dataset skews heavily toward non-explicit content, which reflects either Spotify's catalog composition or the dataset's sampling method.

Methods

The project followed a two-phase approach. In Phase 1 (Dataset Reconnaissance), we loaded and explored the dataset, identified data types, produced summary statistics, and flagged data quality issues — including tracks with impossible zero values for tempo and duration. In Phase 2 (Data Wrangling), we engineered two new features: duration_min (track length in minutes) and grooviness_score (average of danceability and energy). We then filtered the dataset into meaningful subsets — high-popularity tracks, highly danceable tracks, and "banger" tracks that are both popular and danceable.

Interesting Observations

  • The Beatles, George Jones, and Stevie Wonder are the most represented artists — suggesting classic and legacy artists dominate catalog depth on Spotify.
  • The dataset contains exactly 1,000 tracks per genre — a perfectly balanced sample ideal for fair cross-genre comparisons.
  • Several tracks have a tempo or duration of zero, which is physically impossible and reflects data collection errors that must be cleaned before modeling.

Next Steps (Part 2)

Part 2 of this project will focus on visualization — exploring how audio features differ across musical styles and whether those differences correlate with popularity. The perfectly balanced genre distribution makes this dataset ideal for fair cross-genre comparisons.
